Historical Significance

November 1935

The Prediction

Yukawa proposed that the strong nuclear force is mediated by an undiscovered particle with mass ~200× the electron mass, deriving the force range from the uncertainty principle ΔEΔt ≈ ℏ.

1937

False Alarm

The muon (μ) was discovered in cosmic rays with the right mass, but it didn't interact strongly with matter — it was a "wrong particle." The true meson remained elusive.

1947

Confirmation

Cecil Powell's group discovered the pion (π) in photographic emulsions exposed to cosmic rays. Its mass (~270 me) and strong interactions perfectly matched Yukawa's prediction. He received the 1950 Nobel Prize.

Why It Matters

Yukawa's work was the first successful prediction of a new fundamental particle from theoretical reasoning alone. It established the concept of force-mediating virtual particles — later extended to photons (EM), W/Z bosons (weak force), and gluons (strong force) in the Standard Model. The key insight — that a force's range is inversely proportional to the mediator's mass — remains foundational to quantum field theory.
